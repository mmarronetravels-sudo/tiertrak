## About this instrument

The Intervention Monitoring Tier 1 Self-Assessment is designed for school-based MTSS teams to use in their own improvement work. It reflects decades of implementation research and the structure of established MTSS fidelity frameworks — the SWPBIS Tiered Fidelity Inventory (TFI), the Reading Tiered Fidelity Inventory (R-TFI), and the Self-Assessment of Problem Solving Implementation (SAPSI-S) — with items and scoring anchors written specifically for the realities of school implementation teams.

### Who completes this assessment

This assessment is completed by a **building-level MTSS Design & Implementation Team** — the team responsible for the school's universal (Tier 1) practices, fidelity, professional development, and family engagement. This is a system-level team focused on how the school's universal supports are built and maintained.

**This is typically a different team than the Student Support Team** (sometimes called an iTeam or Intervention Team), which focuses on tiering decisions and individual student interventions. Membership may overlap in smaller schools, but the questions below ask about the system, not about individual students.

### Scoring

Each item is scored on a 0–2 scale:

- **0 — Not in place.** The practice is absent, or exists only informally / in conversation.
- **1 — Partially in place.** The practice exists but is inconsistent, undocumented, applied by some staff but not all, or missing a key element.
- **2 — Fully in place.** The practice is documented, consistently implemented school-wide, and staff can articulate it.

Scoring anchors are provided for every item so two different team members evaluating the same practice arrive at similar scores.

### Overall score and interpretation

- 26 items × 2 points each = **52 points maximum**
- Overall score is displayed as a percentage: (points earned / 52) × 100

| Band | % Range | Label | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| Green | 80–100% | **Implementing with Fidelity** | Tier 1 is strong enough that most students should be responding. Team focus can shift to Tier 2/3 precision. |
| Yellow | 50–79% | **Partial Implementation** | Foundations are in place but gaps will push students unnecessarily into Tier 2. Prioritize the lowest-scoring domains. |
| Red | 0–49% | **Installing / Exploration** | Universal supports are not yet strong enough to distinguish "needs intervention" from "never received strong core instruction." Invest here before expanding Tier 2/3. |

### Domain structure and equal weighting

| # | Domain | Items | Max raw points |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Team & Infrastructure | 4 | 8 |
| 2 | Universal Academic Instruction | 4 | 8 |
| 3 | Universal Behavior Supports | 4 | 8 |
| 4 | Universal SEL Supports | 4 | 8 |
| 5 | Universal Screening & Interim Assessment | 4 | 8 |
| 6 | Data-Based Decision Making | 4 | 8 |
| 7 | Family Engagement | 3 | 6 |
| 8 | Professional Development & Coaching | 3 | 6 |
| | **Total** | **26** | **52** |

**Domain-level display.** The results dashboard displays each domain's score as a **percentage of its own maximum** so the 3-item and 4-item domains are visually comparable. A school scoring 4/6 in Family Engagement and 5/8 in Screening is displayed as 67% vs. 63% — which is what the team needs to see when prioritizing.

## Domain 1 — Team & Infrastructure

### Item 1.1 — MTSS Design & Implementation Team exists and meets regularly

Is there a designated building-level MTSS Design & Implementation Team (sometimes called a Tier 1 Team or Universal Supports Team) that meets at least monthly during the school year? This is distinct from a Student Support Team that handles individual tiering decisions.

- **0** — No designated system-level MTSS team exists, or the team exists on paper but does not meet.
- **1** — A team exists and meets, but less than monthly, on an inconsistent schedule, or its focus is routinely pulled into individual student cases rather than universal practices.
- **2** — A designated MTSS Design & Implementation Team meets at least monthly on a published schedule, with a clear focus on Tier 1 systems; meetings happen even when no standing topic feels urgent.

**If below 2, consider:** Establish a standing monthly meeting on the school calendar with a published start and end time. The team should include administration, a grade-level or department representative from each level served, and at least one specialist (counselor, school psychologist, or interventionist). Protect the agenda for system-level work — if individual student conversations dominate, they belong on the Student Support Team's agenda instead. Consistency of cadence matters more than length.

### Item 1.2 — Team roles are defined

Does the MTSS team have defined roles (e.g., facilitator, data lead, note-taker, time-keeper) that are documented and consistently filled?

- **0** — No defined roles; meetings run ad hoc or the principal runs everything.
- **1** — Some roles are informally understood but not documented, or roles rotate without a rotation plan.
- **2** — Roles are documented in a team charter or handbook and are filled at every meeting (by role, not by person — a substitute can step in).

**If below 2, consider:** Draft a one-page team roles document listing facilitator, data lead, note-taker, and time-keeper responsibilities. Assign primary and backup people for each role so meetings can still function when someone is absent. Review role assignments at the start of each school year. *(See the ScholarPath Resources section for an MTSS Team Roles template.)*

### Item 1.3 — Written Tier 1 plan or handbook

Is there a written Tier 1 plan or MTSS handbook that describes universal practices and is accessible to all staff?

- **0** — No written plan; Tier 1 practices live in individual teachers' heads.
- **1** — A plan exists but is outdated (>2 years old), incomplete, or not easily accessible to staff.
- **2** — A current written plan is available to all staff (shared drive, handbook, wiki), reviewed annually, and referenced in staff meetings.

**If below 2, consider:** Start small — a 5–10 page document covering school-wide expectations, the discipline flowchart, screening schedule, and data-meeting cadence is more valuable than an exhaustive binder no one reads. Store it where staff already look for other reference documents. Set a yearly review date on the team's calendar. *(See the ScholarPath Resources section for an MTSS Handbook template.)*

### Item 1.4 — Annual MTSS calendar

Does the team publish an annual calendar showing screening windows, data review dates, and Tier 1 PD events?

- **0** — No calendar; events are scheduled reactively.
- **1** — Some events are on the calendar but not all, or the calendar is not shared with staff.
- **2** — A complete annual calendar is published before the school year starts, includes screening windows and data review dates, and is visible to all staff.

**If below 2, consider:** At the end of each school year, block out the next year's calendar: fall/winter/spring screening windows, monthly team meetings, data review days after each screening window, and at least one Tier 1–focused PD session. Share with staff during summer onboarding or the first week of school. *(See the ScholarPath Resources section for an Annual MTSS Calendar template.)*

## Domain 2 — Universal Academic Instruction

### Item 2.1 — Core curriculum aligned to standards

Is there a documented core curriculum in ELA and math aligned to state standards, consistently used across classrooms at each grade level?

- **0** — No adopted core curriculum, or teachers select their own materials without alignment.
- **1** — A curriculum is adopted but implementation varies significantly across classrooms, or it is only partially aligned to standards.
- **2** — A standards-aligned core curriculum is adopted, implemented across all classrooms at each grade level, and pacing is coordinated.

**If below 2, consider:** Confirm the adopted curriculum is current (within the typical 6–8 year adoption cycle) and aligned to state standards. Establish grade-level pacing guides so students moving between classrooms encounter consistent content. If the curriculum is inconsistent across classrooms, schedule walkthroughs to identify the gaps rather than assuming fidelity.

### Item 2.2 — Protected core instructional time

Is there a defined block of core instructional time in ELA and math that is protected from interruption (announcements, pull-outs, assemblies)?

- **0** — Core instruction is frequently interrupted or reduced; no protected block is defined.
- **1** — A protected block exists in theory but is interrupted with some regularity.
- **2** — A protected core block is defined for ELA and math, schedule-wide, and interruptions are rare exceptions that require administrative approval.

**If below 2, consider:** Define minimum daily minutes for core ELA and math at each grade level and put them on the master schedule. Work with front office staff to minimize announcements during those windows. Move pull-out services (speech, ELL, Tier 2/3 interventions) to times that don't conflict with core instruction whenever possible.

### Item 2.3 — Differentiation within Tier 1

Do teachers routinely differentiate core instruction (grouping, scaffolds, tiered tasks) to meet the range of student needs within the classroom?

- **0** — Instruction is delivered whole-group only; no routine differentiation.
- **1** — Some teachers differentiate consistently; others rarely do. No school-wide approach.
- **2** — Differentiation within Tier 1 is an expected practice, supported by PD and coaching, and visible in walkthroughs across classrooms.

**If below 2, consider:** Identify 1–2 high-leverage differentiation practices (small-group instruction during core, tiered questioning, scaffolded graphic organizers) and make them the focus of PD for a semester. Pair PD with coaching cycles — one-shot workshops rarely change classroom practice. Use walkthroughs to check for implementation, not to evaluate teachers. *(See the ScholarPath Resources section for a High-Leverage Tier 1 Practices reference guide.)*

### Item 2.4 — Documented fidelity of core instruction

Is core instructional fidelity monitored through walkthroughs, lesson plan review, or similar routine?

- **0** — No routine monitoring of core instruction beyond formal evaluation.
- **1** — Monitoring happens inconsistently or is limited to formal teacher evaluation cycles.
- **2** — A non-evaluative walkthrough or lesson review process runs on a defined cadence (e.g., monthly) with aggregated results discussed by the team.

**If below 2, consider:** Design a short (5–10 minute) walkthrough look-fors tool focused on 3–4 Tier 1 practices. Keep it non-evaluative — the goal is system-level data, not individual teacher ratings. Rotate walkthroughs across grade levels and subjects so the team sees the whole picture.

## Domain 3 — Universal Behavior Supports

### Item 3.1 — School-wide expectations are defined

Are 3–5 positively-stated, school-wide behavior expectations defined and agreed upon by staff?

- **0** — No school-wide expectations exist, or each classroom sets its own.
- **1** — School-wide expectations exist but are not consistently known or used by staff.
- **2** — 3–5 positively-stated expectations (e.g., Be Safe, Be Respectful, Be Responsible) are defined, staff can name them, and they are used consistently.

**If below 2, consider:** Convene a representative staff group to draft 3–5 expectations, stated positively (what to do, not what not to do). Get input from students and families before finalizing. Keep the list short — schools that define 8–10 expectations usually find staff can't remember them all.

### Item 3.2 — Expectations are explicitly taught and posted

Are the school-wide expectations explicitly taught to students and visibly posted in classrooms and common areas?

- **0** — Expectations are not taught or posted.
- **1** — Expectations are posted in some areas but not taught, or taught only at the start of the year and not revisited.
- **2** — Expectations are explicitly taught in the first weeks of school, re-taught after breaks and in response to data, and posted in all classrooms and common areas with location-specific examples (hallway, cafeteria, etc.).

**If below 2, consider:** Develop a behavior expectations matrix showing what each expectation looks like in each school setting (classroom, hallway, cafeteria, playground, bus). Build short (10–15 minute) lessons for the first two weeks of school and schedule "booster" lessons after winter and spring breaks. Print the matrix for every classroom and common area.

### Item 3.3 — School-wide acknowledgment system

Is there a school-wide system for acknowledging students who demonstrate the expectations?

- **0** — No consistent acknowledgment system; individual teachers use their own approaches or none.
- **1** — An acknowledgment system exists but is used inconsistently (some staff, some settings).
- **2** — A school-wide acknowledgment system (tickets, shout-outs, recognition routines) is used by all staff in all settings, tied explicitly to the school-wide expectations.

**If below 2, consider:** A working acknowledgment system has three ingredients that matter more than what token you hand out:

- **Specificity.** The acknowledgment names the expectation being reinforced — *"Thanks for being respectful, you held the door for the kindergartners"* — rather than generic praise like *"good job."* Specific acknowledgment teaches the expectation each time it's used; generic praise doesn't.
- **Equity of distribution.** Pull a week's worth of acknowledgments and check whether they reach the full range of students, or cluster on a small group. The students who most need adult validation are often the ones least likely to earn tokens under a purely compliance-based system. Redesign to catch effort and improvement, not just compliance.
- **Universal staff use.** Every adult — including cafeteria staff, bus drivers, paraprofessionals, and office staff — uses the system in every setting. An acknowledgment system that only "lives" in classrooms leaves the settings where conflict often starts unsupported.

Most acknowledgment systems fail on the second point. The specific mechanism matters much less than getting these three ingredients right.

### Item 3.4 — Discipline flowchart with defined classroom-managed vs. office-managed behaviors

Is there a documented discipline flowchart that distinguishes classroom-managed from office-managed behaviors, with defined response steps for each?

- **0** — No flowchart; responses to behavior vary by staff member and situation.
- **1** — A flowchart exists but is not consistently used, or the classroom-managed vs. office-managed distinction is unclear in practice.
- **2** — A documented flowchart defines which behaviors are classroom-managed and which require office referral, with defined response steps at each level; staff apply it consistently.

**If below 2, consider:** Draft a one-page flowchart with both categories and response steps defined. The structure might look like this:

> **Classroom-managed behaviors** (minor disruption, off-task, mild disrespect, dress-code issue):
> 1. Non-verbal redirect
> 2. Private re-teach of the expectation
> 3. Brief conference with the student plus family contact
> 4. If a pattern emerges across days, request Student Support Team consultation
>
> **Office-managed behaviors** (fighting, weapons, drugs, harassment targeting a protected class, sustained defiance after classroom steps, significant property damage):
> Office referral → administrator investigation → consequences per the student code of conduct → family contact → consideration for tier change if a pattern emerges.

Train all staff, including paraprofessionals and supervisors of common areas, on the flowchart. Review office discipline referral data quarterly to check whether it's being applied consistently — if ODRs spike after the flowchart is introduced, it often means staff are finally referring behaviors that had previously been tolerated inconsistently, which is progress, not a problem. *(See the ScholarPath Resources section for a sample Discipline Flowchart.)*

## Domain 4 — Universal SEL Supports

### Item 4.1 — SEL curriculum or structured approach

Is there an adopted SEL curriculum or structured approach (advisory, morning meeting, explicit lessons) delivered to all students?

- **0** — No structured SEL approach; any SEL is informal or teacher-dependent.
- **1** — A curriculum or approach is adopted but delivered inconsistently across classrooms or grade levels.
- **2** — A curriculum or structured approach is adopted, delivered to all students on a defined cadence (e.g., weekly), and aligned to a competency framework (CASEL or similar).

**If below 2, consider:** Identify which CASEL competencies your school will prioritize (self-awareness, self-management, social awareness, relationship skills, responsible decision-making). Select a curriculum or structure (morning meeting, advisory, standalone lessons) that delivers those competencies weekly. Ensure every classroom is delivering it, not just interested teachers.

### Item 4.2 — SEL competencies integrated into daily routines

Are SEL competencies reinforced in daily routines beyond the dedicated SEL block (e.g., restorative practices, check-ins, explicit skill coaching)?

- **0** — SEL only exists during the dedicated SEL block, if at all.
- **1** — Some teachers integrate SEL into daily routines; others do not.
- **2** — SEL integration is an expected daily practice (e.g., morning check-in, closing circle, restorative conversations after conflict), visible across classrooms.

**If below 2, consider:** Pick 1–2 daily routines that reinforce SEL — a 2-minute morning check-in, a closing circle, restorative conversations after conflict — and make them expected practice school-wide. These don't require curriculum; they require consistency.

### Item 4.3 — Staff-student relationship-building practices

Are there structures that ensure every student has at least one caring, consistent adult relationship at school?

- **0** — No intentional structure; relationships form by chance.
- **1** — Some structures exist (advisory, mentorship) but don't reach all students.
- **2** — A structure (advisory, check-in/check-out, looping, mentorship) is in place that ensures every student has a consistent adult connection, and the team reviews whether any student is "unknown" to all staff.

**If below 2, consider:** Run a simple exercise at a staff meeting: post every student's name and ask staff to initial the ones they know well. Students without initials, or with only one, are the focus. Build structures (advisory, lunch bunches, mentorship) that close those gaps. The goal isn't elaborate programming — it's ensuring no student is invisible.

### Item 4.4 — Trauma-informed / culturally responsive practices

Do staff receive training and use practices that are trauma-informed and culturally responsive?

- **0** — No training or intentional practice in these areas.
- **1** — Some training has happened but it hasn't translated into consistent classroom practice.
- **2** — Staff have had recurring training (not a one-off) and team members can name specific trauma-informed or culturally responsive practices in use, supported by coaching.

**If below 2, consider:** One-off PD rarely changes practice in these areas. Look for training providers or internal coaches who offer recurring engagement over a semester or year. Focus first on what staff can *do differently* — greeting students at the door, offering regulation options, examining the cultural relevance of classroom materials — rather than on frameworks alone.

## Domain 5 — Universal Screening & Interim Assessment

### Item 5.1 — Academic interim assessment is conducted for all students

Do all students participate in interim assessments in reading (and math, where applicable to grade levels served) at least twice per year?

- **0** — No universal academic interim assessment, or assessment is done only for students already flagged.
- **1** — Academic interim assessment is conducted but not for all students, or only once per year.
- **2** — All students participate in interim assessments in reading (and math where appropriate) at least twice per year; coverage is verified.

**If below 2, consider:** Select a validated interim assessment appropriate for your grade levels — for example DIBELS, STAR, aimsweb, mCLASS, or NWEA MAP Growth. Schedule fall and spring windows at minimum; add a winter window if feasible. Verify coverage — new students, students absent during the window, and students pulled for other services often get missed. *(Intervention Monitoring's Universal Screener Upload can help surface coverage gaps.)*

### Item 5.2 — Behavior / SEL screening is conducted for all students

Are all students screened in behavior or SEL at least once per year?

- **0** — No behavior/SEL screening conducted.
- **1** — Behavior/SEL screening happens for some students (e.g., teacher nomination only) but is not universal.
- **2** — All students are screened in behavior or SEL at least once per year using a validated instrument (e.g., BESS, SAEBRS, DESSA).

**If below 2, consider:** Behavior/SEL screening is the most commonly skipped universal screening. Teacher-rated screeners (SAEBRS, BESS Teacher Form) can be completed in 1–3 minutes per student and catch internalizing concerns (anxiety, withdrawal) that office discipline referrals miss entirely. Start with a single fall administration and expand from there.

### Item 5.3 — Cut scores and decision rules are documented

Are cut scores and decision rules for flagging students "at risk" documented and consistently applied?

- **0** — No documented cut scores; flagging decisions are made case-by-case.
- **1** — Cut scores exist (from the assessment publisher) but the team's decision rules for action are inconsistent.
- **2** — Cut scores and team decision rules (e.g., "below the 25th percentile triggers a Tier 2 conversation") are documented and applied consistently.

**If below 2, consider:** Write down your decision rules. "Below the 25th percentile on the reading assessment triggers a Tier 2 consideration" is a decision rule. "We'll look at it" is not. Document the rules where the team can see them at every data meeting. Revisit rules annually — not every session, or they become meaningless.

### Item 5.4 — Assessment results are reviewed by the team promptly

Does the team review assessment results within 2–3 weeks of each administration, and are decisions documented?

- **0** — Results are not systematically reviewed by the team.
- **1** — Results are reviewed eventually, but the delay is often long enough that the data is stale before action is taken.
- **2** — Data review meetings are scheduled within 2–3 weeks of each assessment window; decisions about individual students and system-level patterns are documented.

**If below 2, consider:** Put data review dates on the annual calendar at the same time you schedule the assessment windows. Review at both levels: individual students (who needs follow-up) and system-level patterns (is Tier 1 working for the bulk of students?). If 50%+ of students are below benchmark, the problem is Tier 1, not student-level intervention needs.

## Domain 6 — Data-Based Decision Making

### Item 6.1 — Team reviews multiple data sources at every meeting

Does the team routinely review multiple data sources (attendance, office discipline referrals, grades, assessment results) at each meeting?

- **0** — Data is rarely or never reviewed in meetings; discussions are narrative-only.
- **1** — Some data is reviewed but inconsistently, or the team relies on a single data source (e.g., only ODRs).
- **2** — Multiple data sources are reviewed at every meeting using a standard agenda or protocol.

**If below 2, consider:** Build a standing data agenda into every team meeting — even if it's a single 10-minute slot. Rotate the focus so each data source gets deep attention on some schedule (e.g., attendance in October, ODRs in November, assessment data in December). Standardize how data is presented (same format, same charts) so the team spends time thinking, not interpreting slides.

### Item 6.2 — Protocol for tiering decisions (Tier 1 → Tier 2)

Is there a documented protocol the team follows to decide when a student moves from Tier 1 to Tier 2?

- **0** — Tiering decisions are made informally, or based on teacher request alone.
- **1** — A general approach exists but isn't documented; decisions are inconsistent across students or grade levels.
- **2** — A documented protocol defines the data required, the review steps, and the decision criteria for moving a student to Tier 2.

**If below 2, consider:** Write down the protocol. It might be as simple as: "A student is considered for Tier 2 when assessment data is below the 25th percentile AND one of: grades below C, >10% attendance gaps, >3 ODRs. The team reviews these students at the monthly data meeting using [protocol name]." Documentation makes decisions reviewable; reviewability makes them better over time. *(Intervention Monitoring's 11-step Pre-Referral Form is designed to support a documented Tier 1 → Tier 2 protocol.)*

### Item 6.3 — Tier 1 effectiveness is evaluated at the system level

Does the team evaluate Tier 1 effectiveness at the system level (e.g., are ~80% of students meeting benchmark?), separate from individual student decisions?

- **0** — Tier 1 is not evaluated at the system level; all data conversations are about individual students.
- **1** — Some system-level evaluation happens (e.g., at end-of-year) but not regularly.
- **2** — The team evaluates Tier 1 effectiveness at least twice per year and acts on findings (e.g., adjusts PD focus, curriculum, schedule).

**If below 2, consider:** Add a system-level data review to the fall and spring calendar. Ask: are at least 80% of our students meeting benchmark with Tier 1 alone? If not, the issue is Tier 1, not a shortage of Tier 2/3 services. This conversation is uncomfortable but essential — it's the difference between a school that keeps expanding intervention and one that actually gets better.

### Item 6.4 — Tier 1 decisions are documented and tracked over time

Are team decisions about Tier 1 (curriculum adjustments, PD priorities, schedule changes) documented and revisited?

- **0** — Decisions are made in meetings but not tracked; there's no institutional memory.
- **1** — Some decisions are documented in meeting notes but rarely revisited.
- **2** — Decisions are tracked in a running document with owners and due dates, and revisited at subsequent meetings.

**If below 2, consider:** Add a "decisions & owners" section to every meeting note. Each decision gets a person, a by-when date, and a status. Revisit the list at the next meeting before starting new business. This alone will surface more patterns in Tier 1 implementation than any other single practice.

## Domain 7 — Family Engagement

### Item 7.1 — Families are informed about the MTSS framework

Are families informed about how MTSS works at the school and their role in it?

- **0** — Families are not informed about MTSS as a framework.
- **1** — Some communication exists (one-time letter, handbook mention) but families don't know what MTSS means at the school.
- **2** — Families receive clear, accessible communication about MTSS at enrollment and recurring points; communication is in relevant languages.

**If below 2, consider:** Prepare a one-page plain-language explainer of MTSS at your school — what it means if their child is in Tier 1, Tier 2, or Tier 3, and how decisions are made. Share at enrollment, at the start of the year, and when a tiering decision is made. Translate into the languages your families speak.

### Item 7.2 — Two-way family communication channels

Does the school have channels for families to provide input and feedback, not just receive it?

- **0** — Communication flows one direction — school to family.
- **1** — Some feedback mechanisms exist but are rarely used or reviewed.
- **2** — Multiple two-way channels exist (surveys, family council, office hours, home visits) and feedback is reviewed and acted on by leadership.

**If below 2, consider:** Pick one two-way channel to strengthen this year rather than launching many at once. A brief end-of-year family survey with 5 questions, actually reviewed by the team, is more valuable than an elaborate family council that meets twice and disbands.

### Item 7.3 — Families understand universal screening and interim assessment results for their student

When universal screening or interim assessments are administered, do families receive clear, accessible information about their student's results and what those results mean?

- **0** — Families don't receive universal assessment results, or results are shared only when a student is flagged for intervention.
- **1** — Results are shared with families but in a format that's hard to interpret (raw scores, percentiles without explanation), or only for flagged students.
- **2** — All families receive their student's results after each administration in a plain-language format that explains what the results mean, how they compare to benchmark, and what the school is doing in response (at Tier 1 or higher as appropriate).

**If below 2, consider:** Design a one-page parent-facing summary template that goes home after each assessment window. Include: what the assessment measures, the student's score, what "at benchmark" looks like for that grade, and what the school does for all students at Tier 1. If the student's score triggers a Tier 2 conversation, say so clearly. Translate into the languages your families speak. Sharing universal results with every family — not just flagged students — is the single biggest lever for family understanding of MTSS, and it signals that Tier 1 data matters for every kid, not just the ones in intervention. *(See the ScholarPath Resources section for a Parent Assessment Results Summary template. Intervention Monitoring's Parent Portal also supports ongoing family visibility into linked students, interventions, and progress logs.)*

## Domain 8 — Professional Development & Coaching

### Item 8.1 — Tier 1 professional development at least annually

Do all staff receive professional development on Tier 1 practices at least annually, aligned to the school's Tier 1 plan?

- **0** — No dedicated Tier 1 PD; any PD is on other topics.
- **1** — PD happens but is one-off, disconnected from the Tier 1 plan, or reaches only some staff.
- **2** — All staff (including paraprofessionals and support staff) receive at least annual PD on Tier 1 practices, coordinated with the MTSS plan.

**If below 2, consider:** Dedicate at least one PD day per year to Tier 1 practices specifically — not generic differentiation or generic SEL, but the actual practices your school has committed to. Include paraprofessionals and support staff. PD that doesn't connect to a documented Tier 1 plan tends to fade; PD that explicitly reinforces the plan sticks.

### Item 8.2 — Coaching or implementation support is available

Is coaching, peer observation, or other implementation support available to staff working on Tier 1 practices?

- **0** — No implementation support beyond initial PD.
- **1** — Support exists but is limited (e.g., only for new teachers or only on request).
- **2** — A coaching structure is in place (instructional coach, peer observation, PLC with observation cycles) that supports all staff in Tier 1 implementation.

**If below 2, consider:** Joyce & Showers (2002) found that professional development alone — workshops and readings without follow-up — produces relatively weak classroom implementation, while PD paired with coaching and in-classroom follow-through produces substantially higher implementation rates. The specific figures in that research have been debated, but the underlying finding is well-established: coaching multiplies the effect of training. If you can't hire a coach, build peer observation cycles into existing PLC time — teachers observe each other on an agreed look-for, then debrief. Low-cost, high-impact, and it builds internal capacity.

### Item 8.3 — New staff onboarding into the MTSS framework

Are new staff (including mid-year hires and long-term substitutes) onboarded into the school's MTSS framework and Tier 1 practices?

- **0** — No formal onboarding into MTSS; new staff learn by watching.
- **1** — Some orientation exists but is inconsistent, or happens only for fall-hire teachers.
- **2** — A documented onboarding process exists for all new staff (including mid-year hires and long-term subs) covering the Tier 1 plan, discipline flowchart, screening schedule, and SEL approach.

**If below 2, consider:** Build a 60–90 minute MTSS onboarding session into your new-hire orientation. Include: the Tier 1 plan, the discipline flowchart, the school-wide expectations, the screening schedule, and who to ask for help. Assign each new hire a mentor for the first semester. Mid-year hires often miss MTSS entirely if onboarding only happens in August.

## Results view — proposed display

When an assessment is submitted, the results page should show:

1. **Overall score** as a percentage with band label and color coding (green/yellow/red).
2. **Domain bar chart** — 8 bars showing each domain's **percentage of its own maximum** so the 3-item and 4-item domains are visually comparable. Bars use the same color coding as the overall score.
3. **Strength and growth areas** — the 2 highest-scoring and 2 lowest-scoring items surfaced at the top.
4. **Prioritized recommendations** — a list of improvement suggestions, grouped by domain and sorted so the lowest-scoring items appear first. Each item's suggestion text is pulled from this document.
5. **Trend view** (after a second assessment exists) — line chart of overall score and per-domain scores across prior assessments.
6. **Print / Export buttons** — see Print and Export section below.

## UX spec — confirmed design decisions

### Permissions

Only users with `can_complete_tier1_assessment = true` can start or edit an assessment. Admins receive this flag automatically. The flag is managed from the Admin → Staff panel. Assessments are visible (read-only) to all staff at the tenant.

### Scoring input

Each item displays as a question with three radio options (0 / 1 / 2) labeled Not in place / Partially in place / Fully in place. The anchors are available as a tooltip or expandable "what does this mean?" toggle on each item.

### Evidence and Notes fields — privacy by design

**Rationale.** A free-text evidence field could easily be misused to record student or staff names, creating a FERPA risk the user may not even recognize they've created. The field is restructured to minimize that surface.

**Important context for this feature specifically.** Intervention Monitoring holds full student records elsewhere in the app — all students in a school are loaded as Tier 1 students to support universal screening, with referrals surfaced as data comes in. That's the right design for an MTSS tool. But it means a user might reasonably wonder: *"if the app already knows about my students, why can't I mention one in a Notes field?"* The answer is that the Tier 1 Self-Assessment is a different kind of data than the student records — it measures the school's systems, not individual students — and mixing individual student context into it creates avoidable PII risk (trend tracking of a system-level score shouldn't be capable of surfacing a specific student's situation from years ago). The field copy below explains this to users directly.

**Two fields per item, both optional:**

**(a) Evidence link (URL field).** A single URL where the team can paste a link to the supporting artifact (handbook in Google Drive, behavior matrix in OneDrive, SEL curriculum on a shared wiki). URLs rarely contain student PII, and the linked resource is stored wherever the school already controls document access.

- Field label: *"Evidence link (optional)"*
- Placeholder: *"https://... (link to handbook, matrix, or other documentation)"*
- Validated client-side to ensure it's a URL.

**(b) Short notes (text field, 300 character limit).** A brief text field for system-level reflection — naming the curriculum, flagging a caveat, recording why the team chose 1 vs. 2.

- Field label: *"Notes about this practice (optional)"*
- Placeholder: *"E.g., 'Adopted Second Step SEL in 2023, delivered weekly grades K–5.' Do not include student or staff names."*
- Character counter visible.

**Help text below both fields:**
> *This assessment tracks school-wide practices, not individual students. Please don't include student or staff names in any field here — even though Intervention Monitoring holds student records elsewhere in the app, this assessment is scoped to system-level reflection. Links to documents, named curricula, and program names are fine.*

### PII detection on the Notes field

**Client-side detection on save (onBlur and on final submit).** Before the note is committed, a client-side check flags patterns that look like names:

- Two consecutive capitalized words that are not at the start of a sentence (e.g., "Marcus Lee").
- A single capitalized word not at the start of a sentence and not matching an allow-list of common non-name capitalized terms (Tier, ELA, Math, SEL, MTSS, IEP, 504, PBIS, STAR, DIBELS, aimsweb, NWEA, MAP, BESS, SAEBRS, DESSA, Second Step, PATHS, CASEL, K–5, K-12, CCSS, ODR, PLC, PD, etc.). This allow-list will be maintained in code and is tenant-agnostic.

**If a match is detected, a confirmation dialog fires:**

> **Please review your note**
>
> This note appears to include what may be a student or staff name. Intervention Monitoring doesn't collect individual names in Tier 1 assessments — it tracks school-wide practices only.
>
> [Edit my note] [Save anyway]

The user can edit or override. If they override, the save goes through. The dialog fires once per save attempt per note to avoid nagging.

**Honest limitations** (documented here so they're not hidden from future reviewers):

- Regex-based detection will miss first names alone ("Marcus has been doing well"). Catching those without huge false-positive rates requires a name list, which would itself be a privacy concern.
- A determined user can defeat detection by paraphrasing.
- This is defense-in-depth, not a guarantee. It reduces accidental PII capture significantly; it doesn't eliminate it.

**Why we are not doing server-side PII detection.** Running name-detection logic against data that may contain names creates its own PII-adjacent surface (detection results, logs). The simpler architectural posture is "reduce the rate of accepting it in the first place" rather than "accept it and try to redact."

### Autosave and partial completion

Each item's response (score + URL + notes) autosaves onBlur (same pattern as the Pre-Referral Form). An assessment can be in state `in_progress` or `completed`.

- Only **one `in_progress` assessment per tenant at a time.** Completing it finalizes the scores and starts a new trend data point.
- The team can leave and return across multiple sittings.
- The dashboard card shows whether there's an `in_progress` assessment ("Resume →") or prompts to start a new one.

### Cadence nudges

No hard-coded schedule. Dashboard messaging:

- No prior assessment: *"Start your first Tier 1 Self-Assessment to baseline your universal supports."*
- Last assessment >6 months ago: *"Your last Tier 1 Self-Assessment was [N months] ago. A fall/spring cadence is recommended."*
- `in_progress` assessment exists: *"You have an assessment in progress. Resume →"*

### Frequency

Unlimited. The trend view handles whatever cadence the school actually uses.

### District-level scope (deferred)

v1 ships as building-level only. The schema will include a `scope` field on the assessment (`building` as the only valid value initially) and a nullable `subject_tenant_id` field so either of the following district modes can be added in a later release without a schema migration:

- A district coordinator completing the same instrument about a specific school (paired observer mode).
- A separate district-facing assessment with its own item bank for districts to assess themselves.

Which district mode (if any) to build will be decided after real user signal from live building-level deployments.

### Future enhancement — data-aware item validation (v2+)

Because Intervention Monitoring already holds the full student population and screener/interim assessment uploads, some items in this assessment could be partially validated against real data. For example, Item 5.1 ("all students participate in interim assessments at least twice per year") could be cross-checked against screener upload history to flag when a self-reported "Full" score may not match the data.

**This is explicitly out of scope for v1** because:

- It introduces an "app is second-guessing the team" dynamic that can undermine trust in the instrument.
- It requires careful UX design for when the data and the self-report disagree.
- A false-positive or false-negative in the validation logic would be a much bigger problem than a rough self-report.

The schema proposal in the next step will remain compatible with a future data-aware mode, but no validation logic ships in v1.

## Data handling — PII and privacy notes

This feature is about **system-level practices, not individual students**. By design, the assessment does not collect student PII.

- No student names, IDs, or demographic data are entered or stored by this feature.
- The only person-identifying field is `completed_by_user_id` (the staff member who submitted the assessment — already a known user in the system).
- Evidence URLs and Notes are subject to the privacy-by-design measures described above (validation, length limits, client-side name detection, user confirmation dialog, explicit anti-PII copy in multiple locations).
- Standard tenant isolation applies: every read and write scoped to `tenant_id`.

## Print and export — two outputs

### Quick print (MVP)

- Triggered from the Results view via "Print results" button.
- Applies print styles to the existing results layout (CSS `@media print`).
- Produces a clean printable page / browser-saved PDF suitable for internal team use, a staff meeting handout, or a quick district check-in.
- Low effort; ships with the MVP.
- Includes: overall score, domain chart, strength/growth items, recommendations, Evidence URLs. Notes text **is** included here (internal team view).

### Formal PDF report (v1.1 release)

Separate feature, shipped after MVP. Generates a structured multi-page PDF suitable for sharing with district leadership, grant reviewers, or accreditation visits. The document is titled **"Intervention Monitoring Tier 1 Self-Assessment"** on first reference and **"Tier 1 Self-Assessment"** thereafter.

- **Cover page:** school name, tenant name, assessment date, completed by, overall score band, FERPA/confidentiality notice. ScholarPath Systems branding.
- **Executive summary:** one-paragraph interpretation of the score plus the 3–5 highest-priority recommendations.
- **Domain breakdown:** each domain's score with a short interpretation.
- **Full results table:** every item, its score, and any Evidence URL.
- **Improvement plan:** all recommendations for items below Full, organized by domain, phrased as actions.
- **Appendix:** scoring methodology, anchor definitions.
- **Footer:** generated by ScholarPath Intervention Monitoring on [date].

**Notes text is deliberately omitted from the formal PDF.** Even with client-side name detection, short notes carry residual PII risk. A formal report that gets forwarded to a district email address, attached to a grant application, or saved to a shared drive multiplies that risk. The formal PDF includes Evidence URLs (which link to school-controlled documents) but not the free-text notes. Users who want their notes in a printed document can use the quick-print option, which stays internal to the team.

This is a defense-in-depth decision consistent with FERPA data minimization principles — the formal report is designed to be safe to forward.
